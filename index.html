Problem Overview

Credit card fraud presents a significant financial challenge due to extreme class imbalance and evolving attack patterns. U.S. consumers lost over $12.5 billion to fraud in 2024, up 25% year-over-year. This study evaluates multiple machine learning paradigms to identify models best suited for real-world deployment.

$12.5B
Annual Fraud Losses (U.S.)

Credit card fraud is the most prevalent form of identity theft, with losses growing 25% year over year.

71%
Card-Not-Present Fraud

E-commerce and phone transaction fraud dominates, making detection harder as physical card theft declines.

Research Question

Which machine learning approach (traditional, ensemble, or deep learning) provides the most effective fraud detection on highly imbalanced financial data, as measured by precision, recall, F1-score, ROC-AUC, and computational efficiency?

Research Objectives
  • Compare the performance of five machine learning algorithms for credit card fraud detection.
  • Evaluate how effectively each model handles highly imbalanced data where fraud represents only 0.172% of transactions.
  • Determine which approach is most suitable for real-world deployment by examining accuracy and computational requirements.
  • Analyze the trade-offs between model accuracy and computational efficiency to provide practical guidance for financial institutions.

Methodology

Five models spanning three paradigms were trained on SMOTE-balanced data and evaluated on the original imbalanced test set using precision, recall, F1-score, ROC-AUC, and training time.

What the Data Tells Us

Increased model complexity did not correspond to improved performance on this dataset.

üèÜ Ensemble Methods Perform Best

Random Forest and XGBoost consistently outperform both traditional and deep learning models on structured tabular data. Random Forest achieved the highest F1-score (84.10%) and precision (84.54%).

‚ö° Efficiency vs. Performance

XGBoost offers the best balance between computational efficiency and predictive performance, training in 15.84 seconds while achieving the highest ROC-AUC (97.60%). Ideal for production environments.

‚ùå Deep Learning Underperforms

Deep NN required 499 seconds yet produced the lowest ROC-AUC (96.82%). Structured tabular data with PCA features does not benefit from deep architectures.

‚öñÔ∏è Precision-Recall Trade-off

Financial institutions must choose: minimize false alarms (Random Forest) or maximize fraud detection (XGBoost). No single model optimizes both simultaneously.

üìä SMOTE Validated

All models detected 80 to 92% of fraud from a 0.172% fraud rate. Without SMOTE, models would predict "legitimate" every time: 99.83% accuracy, zero fraud caught.

üéØ Simplicity Wins

The fastest model (XGBoost, 16s) beat the slowest (Deep NN, 499s). More sophisticated architectures did not yield superior performance in this context.
